    Insider’s guide to investing $5k

    Editor Iamaneditor
    Coney Island

    Editor
    Iamaneditor

    Coney Island

    This content was created in partnership with BetaShares.

    If you’ve been diligently squirrelling away your extra cash despite the challenges of modern pandemic life, that’s excellent. But it’s time to make it work harder, so you can enjoy even greater reward for your efforts.

    Even a high-interest savings account isn’t going to make a good return on a smaller sum, so what to do rather than let it languish?

    We asked some money experts to share their tips on how they would invest $5000 to make a bigger impact on your financial future.

    PAY IT DOWN

    Money expert Effie Zahos says one of the first things people should do is first look if there is ‘bad’ debt they can clear away.

    “Get that monkey off your back,” she says. “And then set up a double whammy – by setting up an emergency fund while also paying down your home loan.”

    Ms Zahos says to remember that the interest on home loans is calculated on the daily balance and charged to the loan account monthly in arrears.

    “Take advantage of this fact, because you can reduce the daily balance, even by just $1, and you will save on both term and interest,” she says. “Putting $5000 in your mortgage offset would equate to $160 savings in interest on a $500,000 mortgage. Big savings kick in over the life of the loan, so you’d save a massive $7800 in mortgage interest over a 30 year term – and pay it off faster.”

    Opt for an offset account rather than a redraw facility, as offset accounts can be more versatile, says Ms Zahos.

    And don’t forget that adding money to your super fund is also a good way to invest money in the long-term.

    “While super isn’t perfect, it’s important to remember that it is your money,” says Ms Zahos. “For a 30-year-old who pops $5000 into their super, the difference could mean an extra $18,000 in retirement.”

    JUMP INTO INVESTING

    Moving your money out of a savings account, and investing it instead, puts your money to work.

    “It means you do less of the heavy lifting,” says Ms Zahos. “If you had invested $5000 into the top three performing broad-based Aussie equity exchange traded funds then you would’ve earned over $1400 on your investment in just 12 months. Of course, hindsight can be a wonderful thing – last year’s winners can end up being this year’s duds.”

    She says if you’re new to investing, nothing beats the basics.“For me, making money is about buying good-quality assets and holding them for a reasonable amount of time,” Ms Zahos says. “Don’t over-complicate things – there are really only a handful of options you can invest in: yourself, cash, fixed income, property and equities. There’s nothing mysterious about them.”

    And don’t worry if you’re feeling unsure. “If you think FOMO (fear of missing out) is bad, jumping into an investment because you feel you have to – FOJI (fear of jumping in) – can be just as bad,” Ms Zahos warns. “Flip your thinking around. If you don’t invest, you run the risk of not having enough money for a comfy retirement. Get money savvy through education and seek expert help if you need to.”

    INVEST IN YOURSELF

    Covid 19 has shown us that working from home and with flexible hours is not only possible – it can be profitable. Now is a good time to get involved in the gig economy and invest in yourself. Money educator Vanessa Stoykov suggests investing $5000 into your business ideas.

    “It’s amazing what a little bit can do if you invest in yourself,” she says. “I read about a woman who used $8000 to buy supplies to make bath bombs. She was worried she’d lost that money, but now a few years later she’s turning over $2 million dollars – and that initial $8000 got her there.”

    Ms Stoykov says that diversifying income streams makes our financial positions stronger.

    “Covid has allowed more opportunities,” she says. “It’s really cool that we can mix home and work and have a side hustle without huge overheads, like rent or paying wages.”

    Ms Stoykov started her first business aged 26 with $14,000. “When I start something new I ask people would they think: would they use it? Would they buy it? Would they pay for that? And how much? People are willing to give their opinion, that's for sure!”

    And part of the initial outlay might be on a course or learning a new skill, but consider that cost as part of the investment. “Just $5000 allows you to make more income and allows you to have different streams,” she says. “You’ve just got to back yourself – it’s for yourself and for your family.”
